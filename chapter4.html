Chapter 4 · Multiscale ICME + experiment

Does it hold up in a real metal?

Everything so far lived inside a computer. Here the bonds finally meet a real piece of titanium — pulled apart in the lab, cooled to 143 K, and predicted from first principles all the way up.

Scales  DFT → MD → crystal plasticity Codes  VASP · LAMMPS · DAMASK Experiment  cryogenic tension + EBSD Status  ~75% computational · EBSD in progress

There is a quiet anxiety that runs under all computational materials science: does any of this survive contact with a real specimen? A descriptor can be elegant and a DFT number can be converged to the micro-electronvolt, and the metal in your hand can still do something else entirely. Chapter 4 is where I made myself find out — by building a pipeline that carries titanium across every length scale and then testing its predictions against tension experiments I ran myself.

This is ICME — integrated computational materials engineering — the idea that you can chain simulations across scales so each one feeds the next. No single method spans from an electron to a tensile bar. DFT is exquisite but tiny. Crystal plasticity sees whole grains but knows nothing of bonds. The craft is in the hand-offs.

The pipeline, scale by scale

The hard part: what sets the slip resistance?

Titanium deforms through five different slip families, each with its own resistance, and the relative ordering of those resistances is what controls the texture and the anisotropy. My DFT gives the ratios between them — the GSFE hierarchy, prismatic easiest, ⟨c+a⟩ pyramidal hardest. The experiment gives the absolute scale. I fit the crystal-plasticity model under both constraints at once, which is what makes the prediction more than a curve-fit.

The cryogenic angle is not decoration. Titanium gets stronger and changes how it slips as it cools, and that regime — 143 to 298 K — is exactly where LNG and aerospace hardware lives.

The five ways titanium slips

Part of why titanium is hard to model is that it has five distinct slip families plus twinning, and they activate in a strict order. My DFT computes the stacking-fault barrier for every one of them — the relative ordering is what the crystal-plasticity model needs:

Where the experiment comes in — EBSD

A crystal-plasticity simulation is only as honest as the microstructure you feed it. Most studies assume a random texture because it is convenient. I am measuring the real one. EBSD gives me the actual grain orientations, sizes and morphology of my specimens, which become the representative volume DAMASK deforms — real texture in, real anisotropy out.

Honestly where this stands: the computational backbone — the DFT dataset for all five slip families and the MD potential benchmarking — is about three-quarters done. The cryogenic tension data and the EBSD orientation maps are being acquired now, and the EBSD-informed DAMASK runs depend on them. So this chapter is the most actively moving part of the thesis: a finished skeleton waiting on its experimental flesh. The figures here are deliberately schematic for that reason — the measured stress–strain curves and pole figures go in as they arrive.

Why it matters for the rest of the journey

Chapter 4 is the proof that the bond-level story scales all the way to a load frame. But it also taught me something uncomfortable: doing this once, for pure titanium, took the better part of a year of careful, expert, error-prone hand-work — converging DFT, fitting potentials, wiring up DAMASK, debugging cluster jobs. If the pipeline is this powerful and this painful, the obvious question is whether a machine could run it. That question is Chapter 5.
